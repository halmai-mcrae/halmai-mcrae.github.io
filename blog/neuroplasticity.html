Neuroplasticity & Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity:

Neuroplasticity refers to your brain’s ability to adapt when it recognises the need to. We used to believe that the brain became fixed after a certain age, but more recent research has revealed that the brain possesses an amazing capacity to develop new abilities, create new connections and never stops changing in response to learning.

Understanding the concept of neuroplasticity has an overall positive effect on motivation, achievement, and brain activity. It can help people develop a growth mindset rather than fixed mindset(which I’ll elaborate on later) which can encourage approaching problems or things they find difficult, with curiosity and patience rather than internalised negativity.

I found it extremely encouraging to understand that, with some time and patience, the brain can actually be rewired- which may help with learning, certain mental health symptoms and protect against cognitive decline. I first delved into neuroplasticity in EDA’s prep curriculum, and am so happy that it was presented there. I think without understanding the principles, I would have begun Foundations very differently.

It is so important for people to understand that the capabilities and knowledge they already possess, is not what they are limited to.

Some ways to increase your neuroplasticity:

  • Tap into existing pathways: Do further research about things you're intrested or involved in.
  • Keep practising: Repeating an activity builds stronger, more hardwired connections in the brain.
  • Video games:Yes, really!
  • Exercise regularly: Physical exercise boosts the part of the brain responsible for motor control and learning.
  • Remind yourself about neuroplasticity and how the brain works!

Growth Mindset:

People who believe their skills can be developed (through hard work, good strategies, and patience) have what’s known as a growth mindset.

They tend to achieve more than those with fixed mindset (those who believe their skills are limited to what they are already good at).

Things that surprised me in this exploration:

Learning about how much mindset plays into achieving was extremely eye-opening and encouraging. I found the Ted Talk included in Sprint 4 resources, about educating students on growth mindset and “The Yet, or Not Yet” method of grading to be really moving and indicative of how important it is in correlation to learning.

How I will be implementing growth mindset into my learning:

In many ways I have been, but there is always room for improvement. I definitely struggle with confidence and the research on growth mindset indicates that communicating or discussing newly learned things can increase confidence in your abilities. I also need to understand that struggling is normal and not to be villainized as it’s the first step to learning.
